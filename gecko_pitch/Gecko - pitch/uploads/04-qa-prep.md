# Q&A Prep — The 10 Hardest Questions

For the panel review (post-shortlisting). These are the questions that separate "good demo" from "wins on stage." Memorize the *first sentence* of each answer — that's the part that decides whether the conversation goes well.

The hardest part of Q&A isn't knowing the answer. It's not over-explaining. **Each first-sentence answer is your real answer. Everything after is optional context.**

---

## 1. "Aren't you just a wrapper around x402?"

**Sharp answer:** *No. We're the first product where the buyer is an agent and the beneficiary is a person. x402 is the only protocol that makes that buying possible — but the product is the agent-to-human knowledge transfer, not the protocol layer.*

**If they push:** MCPay sells per-call infrastructure. We sell decisions a founder acts on for 90 days. Different unit, different category. The same x402 rails carry both, but we're not competing for the same customer.

---

## 2. "Why won't OpenAI/Anthropic just build this in 6 months?"

**Sharp answer:** *Because the moat isn't the LLM call — it's the encoded judgment in how we orchestrate five specialist agents around a citation-grounded knowledge base. OpenAI ships horizontal capabilities. We ship vertical judgment about a specific category.*

**If they push:** They'd also have to build the x402 integration, the Solana wallet flows, the ClawRouter compatibility, and convince founders to install a chat app. Or they could let us do that and partner. We're betting they'll partner — they've never been good at vertical products.

---

## 3. "$20 a session is too cheap for the value, $20 is too expensive for ChatGPT replacement. Which are you?"

**Sharp answer:** *We're not pricing against ChatGPT. We're pricing against the alternative: hiring a senior product person for $5,000 to validate an idea, or shipping six months of the wrong product. $20 is calibrated to the decision, not the compute.*

**If they push:** Pro at $75 USDC competes with junior consultant work. We can defend that price because the output is structured, cited, and reusable for 90 days.

---

## 4. "Show me a session output. Is it actually better than what GPT-4o-with-search produces?"

**Sharp answer:** *Yes — by being specifically grounded in the founder's chosen sources, with citations that resolve, structured to act on. Let me show you the difference side by side.*

**Then actually show them.** Have a session output ready, *and* the same idea run through ChatGPT Search. The difference must be visible — citation count, specificity, action-orientation.

**If you can't honestly show this difference, you have a deeper problem and the right answer is to acknowledge:** *"That's our core risk. Phase 13 of our build plan is two full days of prompt engineering specifically to make this gap obvious. We're treating this as the moat."*

---

## 5. "Your moat is prompts. Prompts are not defensible."

**Sharp answer:** *The defensible thing isn't the prompts — it's the iteration loop. Prompts get cloned in a weekend. The judgment of which prompts to write, after testing 100 variants on real founder feedback, takes months and gets sharper as we run more sessions. That's the loop.*

**If they push:** Same reason Notion can't be cloned by copying their templates. The product is the accumulated judgment about what a good template looks like, not any specific template.

---

## 6. "Who's actually using this? What's your traction?"

**Honest answer (don't dodge):** *V1 of the original creator-economy version is live. The agent-native pivot to Builder Bootstrap is two weeks old. We're submitting to validate with judges and the SuperteamBR community before we pursue paying users — paying users come from the accelerator, not from cold launch.*

**Don't fake traction.** Judges spot it instantly. The honest answer is the strong answer, because it pairs with: *"We pivoted because the user research told us creators wouldn't claim. We listened. Now we need ten paying builder sessions to validate the new positioning, and we're scoping that as the first 30 days post-Colosseum."*

---

## 7. "Why Solana specifically? You could do this on Base or Stellar."

**Sharp answer:** *Because Solana's ecosystem made this possible — frames.ag, ClawRouter, and the SuperteamBR community are all Solana-native. The product is composable specifically because of where it's built. We could port to Base, but we'd lose the ecosystem we depend on for distribution.*

**If they push:** Long-term, x402 is multi-chain (we already accept Base USDC as a fallback). But for V1, Solana is where the ecosystem is, where our users are, and where the partnerships are.

---

## 8. "What stops a creator from feeling exploited when their content trains your output?"

**Sharp answer:** *Nothing — until V2, when we add visible attribution and a claim flow. V1 cites every source URL in every document. A creator can see they were cited and link it for their own credibility. V2 adds the option to claim earnings. We're explicit about this trajectory; we don't pretend V1 solves it.*

**If they push:** We don't expect most creators to claim, even at V2. The honest market signal from our research was: creators value attribution more than micropayments. So V2 leads with attribution and treats earnings as an opt-in extension.

---

## 9. "How do you make money? $20-75 a session is a business but not a venture business."

**Sharp answer:** *Three layers. V1: paid sessions to validate that builders pay for grounded research. V2: B2B — accelerators and VC funds pay $1,000–5,000 per portfolio company for batched validation reports. V3: API for other agentic frameworks who route their users' research needs to us. The session is the wedge, not the business model.*

**If they push:** Compare to Notion's path — personal plan was the wedge, team plans became the business. Same shape: we're building distribution before we monetize the highest-value customer.

---

## 10. "What's the riskiest assumption in your product, that if wrong, kills it?"

**Sharp answer (this is the question that earns trust):** *That founders care enough about validation to actually pay for it. Most founders don't validate before building — they intuit and ship. We're betting that for $20, the friction of validation drops below the friction of intuition. If we're wrong about that price-elasticity threshold, the wedge fails and we have to reposition toward higher-value customers (accelerators, VC firms) earlier than V2 plans.*

**If they push:** That's why pricing is what we'll iterate on first. $20 is our hypothesis. If sessions stall, we'll test $5, $50, $200 to find the elastic point. The product works; the price is the unknown.

---

## Two questions you might also get, but rarely

### "Why aren't you using ChatGPT instead of building this?"
*Because ChatGPT has no concept of a session, no grounding contract, no agent-native payment, and produces output a founder can't act on without restructuring. Different product entirely.*

### "Why did you pivot? Original idea bad?"
*Original idea (creator economy on-chain) was directionally right but had a fatal user-research finding: creators wouldn't claim payments at the rate the model required. The founder pain on the builder side was stronger than the creator pain. We followed the strongest pain. That's the pivot.*

---

## How to handle a question you don't have a sharp answer for

**The lethal failure mode is hand-waving.** If a judge asks something you genuinely don't know:

> "I don't have a confident answer to that yet. Here's what I know and don't know..."

Then state the boundary honestly. Judges respect this 10x more than fake confidence. Most founders fake-confident their way through Q&A; saying "I don't know" with composure is differentiation.

**Never say "good question."** It's a tell. The judge knows it's a good question — that's why they asked.

**Never start a question with "Yeah, so..."** Time-stalling. Start with the answer.

---

## Practice protocol

Before the panel review:

1. Have someone (your co-founder, a friend, ChatGPT in adversarial mode) ask you each of these 10 questions cold. Time your first sentence. If it's longer than 12 seconds, you're rambling — tighten.
2. Record yourself answering. Watch the playback. Cut every "um," "like," and "you know" — they signal uncertainty.
3. Memorize the first sentence of each answer verbatim. Improvise after that.
4. The day of the review: skim this doc once. Don't re-memorize. Trust the muscle memory.
